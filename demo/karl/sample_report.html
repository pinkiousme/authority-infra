Most oil and gas companies at $75M to $150M are running the same revenue strategy.

Stay close to the network. Show up at the right conferences. Wait for the RFQ to land in the inbox.

That works until a major account goes quiet. Then three months pass and the team is chasing work instead of building it.

Not a market problem. Not a product problem.

A timing problem.

The companies that break through that revenue band share one characteristic: they map capital deployment in the sector before the procurement cycle opens. They are already in conversation with the decision-makers 60 to 90 days before a project reaches the RFQ desk.

In refining and petrochemical, those 60 days are the entire competitive window.

Relationships get you the meeting. Market intelligence about where capital is forming determines whether you are in the right room before the budget is allocated.

Most companies at this stage have the relationships.

The missing piece is a structured process for identifying which accounts are entering a capital expenditure window, and getting positioned before the formal procurement cycle starts.

That is what a revenue development function provides at this stage. Not more networking. A system for knowing where capital is moving six months before it moves.
