Content Extraction
The Post-Call Extraction Prompt
Turn any client call into 3 ready-to-draft LinkedIn posts before you close your notes.
OPEN
When to use Run this within 30 minutes of ending a client call, discovery call, or advisory session — while the content is still fresh. Paste in your raw notes or a rough transcript summary. Works with even 3–4 bullet points.
The Prompt
I just finished a client call. Here are my rough notes: [PASTE YOUR CALL NOTES OR A SHORT SUMMARY — even 3-4 bullet points works] My role: [e.g. Fractional CMO / GTM Advisor / CRO] My ICP (who I work with): [e.g. B2B SaaS founders, Series A-B] From these notes, identify: 1. The most specific insight or diagnosis I gave the client 2. The most interesting tension or problem we uncovered 3. The framework or approach I reached for instinctively Then turn each of those into a LinkedIn post opening — just the first 3 lines (the hook). Not the full post. I want to see which angle is worth developing. Format your response as three numbered hooks, each under 180 characters, followed by one sentence explaining why that angle would resonate with my ICP.
Pro Tip Pick the hook that makes you think "I've never quite said it that way before." That friction is the signal — it means you've articulated something that was previously only in your head.

The Objection Miner
Turn a sales objection into two high-converting post angles. The best objections are always content.
OPEN
When to use Any time a prospect pushes back on your offer, raises a doubt, or asks a question that made you work for the answer. Run this immediately after a discovery call or sales conversation while the objection is still clear in your mind.
The Prompt
A prospect just raised this objection with me: "[PASTE THE EXACT OBJECTION OR DESCRIBE IT IN A SENTENCE]" My role: [Fractional CMO / GTM Advisor / CRO / etc.] What I actually do: [1-2 sentences on your offer] How I handled it in the call: [what you said back — rough is fine] This objection tells me something about how my ICP thinks about this problem. I want to turn it into content. Give me two LinkedIn post angles: - Angle A: A teaching post that addresses the root misconception behind the objection without ever mentioning the objection directly - Angle B: An opinion post that takes the contrarian position on whatever assumption the objection reveals For each angle: write the hook (first 2-3 lines only) and a one-sentence brief explaining what the body of the post would cover.
Pro Tip Your best-performing posts are usually answers to objections your paying clients never raised — because your content already handled it. This prompt closes that loop systematically.

Content Extraction
The Framework Post Builder
Turn a methodology you use with clients into a structured LinkedIn teaching post. Works with any framework, model, or diagnostic you reach for regularly.
OPEN
When to use When you catch yourself explaining the same framework to multiple clients. If you've said it three times, it belongs on LinkedIn. This prompt turns the in-your-head version into a structured, shareable post.
The Prompt
I use a framework or approach with clients that I've never written about publicly. Here's a rough description of it: Name (if I have one for it): [or write "unnamed"] What problem it solves: [describe the client situation it addresses] The steps or components: [list them out roughly — doesn't need to be polished] What changes for the client after using it: [the outcome or shift it produces] A real example (anonymised is fine): [describe a client situation where you used it] Write a LinkedIn teaching post based on this framework. Requirements: - Open with the problem it solves, not the name of the framework - Explain it in 3-5 clear steps, each actionable and specific - Include the anonymised client example as the proof point - Close with a declarative statement about what changes when this is applied — no question CTA - Keep paragraphs to 2-3 sentences maximum - No exclamation marks. No em dashes. No emojis.
Pro Tip After the post is drafted, ask Claude: "What would someone need to believe for this framework to feel irrelevant to them?" — that answer is your next post.

Post Writing
The Contrarian Take Generator
Turn a strong opinion you hold about your industry into a short, declarative opinion post. No hedging. No both-sides framing.
OPEN
When to use When you read a LinkedIn post from someone in your space and your instinct is "that's wrong" or "that's incomplete." Your disagreement is the post. Use this to sharpen it into something worth publishing.
The Prompt
I have a contrarian view I want to turn into a LinkedIn opinion post. My view: [state your opinion plainly — one sentence, no hedging] The common belief I'm pushing against: [what most people in my space believe or do] Why I believe this: [your experience or evidence — rough notes are fine] Who I want to reach: [your ICP — e.g. fractional CMOs, GTM advisors] Write a short LinkedIn opinion post (under 900 characters) that: - Opens with the opinion stated directly in the first line — no build-up - Backs it with 2-3 sentences of evidence or experience - Names the cost of the opposite belief in one specific sentence - Ends with a single declarative statement — not a question - Sounds like a senior practitioner, not a content creator - Zero em dashes, zero exclamation marks, zero emojis
Pro Tip Opinion posts with no CTA often outperform posts that ask for a comment — because they feel like a genuine statement, not a lead generation exercise. Let the post land. Your profile does the rest.

The Story Post Extractor
Turn a client situation, a conversation, or a moment from your consulting work into a story post with a transferable lesson.
OPEN
When to use When something interesting happened in a client engagement — a breakthrough, a failure, an unexpected discovery, a moment where your diagnosis turned out to be different from what you expected. Write the story while it's still fresh.
The Prompt
I want to write a LinkedIn story post about a client situation. Here are the details (I'll anonymise the client): The situation: [describe what was happening — what they hired you for, what the problem looked like from the outside] The tension or unexpected discovery: [what you found when you dug in — the part that surprised even you] What you did or recommended: [what you actually changed or advised] The outcome: [what happened as a result — specific if possible] The lesson that's transferable: [what does this mean for someone reading this who is in a similar situation] Write a LinkedIn story post that: - Opens mid-scene — drops the reader directly into the moment, not the background - Keeps the client situation entirely anonymous but specific enough to be credible - Builds toward the lesson naturally rather than announcing it early - Closes with a declarative insight, not a question - Paragraphs of 2-3 sentences maximum - No em dashes, no exclamation marks
Pro Tip The best story posts feel slightly uncomfortable to write — because they reveal how you actually think, not just what you recommend. That discomfort is the signal you're writing something worth reading.

The Belief Shift Builder
Reframe one wrong assumption your ICP holds about their problem. The most powerful posts change how someone thinks, not just what they know.
OPEN
When to use When you notice the same misdiagnosis recurring across multiple clients or prospects. If they all think the problem is X and you know it's actually Y, that gap is a belief shift post. One per month maximum — these posts need weight behind them.
The Prompt
I want to write a belief shift post for LinkedIn — a post that moves my ICP from a wrong assumption to a more accurate diagnosis. The wrong assumption most of my ICP holds: [what they believe the problem is] What the problem actually is: [your reframe — the correct diagnosis] Why they hold the wrong belief: [what led them there — usually surface evidence that points to the wrong cause] What changes when they adopt the right belief: [the practical shift in what they do or stop doing] My ICP: [fractional CMOs / GTM advisors / etc.] Write a LinkedIn belief shift post that: - Opens by naming the wrong belief plainly — no hedging - Shows why intelligent people hold this belief (validate without condescending) - Introduces the correct diagnosis with a clear structural distinction - Shows what becomes possible with the right framing - Closes with one declarative sentence that lands the shift - Never uses "Here's the thing:" or "Let me share..." - No em dashes, no exclamation marks, no closing question
Pro Tip The line "why intelligent people hold this belief" is the most important part. If you skip it, the post sounds condescending. If you include it, the reader feels seen — which is the moment they start trusting you.

The Hook Rewriter
Take any existing post draft and get five completely different opening lines — each using a different scroll-stopping technique.
OPEN
When to use When you've written a post but the first line feels flat. Or when a post underperformed and you suspect the hook was the problem. Run this and replace the opening — everything else stays.
The Prompt
Here is a LinkedIn post I've written: [PASTE YOUR FULL POST DRAFT] The hook (first 1-2 lines) is weak. Write 5 alternative hooks for this post, each using a different technique: Hook 1 — THE CONTRADICTION: Open with a statement that contradicts a common belief related to this topic Hook 2 — THE SPECIFIC NUMBER: Lead with a precise number, data point, or timeframe that makes the reader curious Hook 3 — THE NAMED SITUATION: Open by describing a specific scenario the reader will recognise themselves in Hook 4 — THE DIAGNOSIS: State the root cause of the problem directly, in the first line, without build-up Hook 5 — THE COST: Open by naming what someone loses or leaves on the table by not knowing what this post reveals Requirements for all 5: - Under 180 characters each - No em dashes, no exclamation marks, no emojis - Do not open with "I" - Sound like a senior practitioner, not a content creator After the 5 hooks, recommend which one to lead with and explain in one sentence why.
Pro Tip The hook accounts for roughly 80% of a post's reach — because the algorithm measures "see more" clicks in the first 30 minutes. If those are low, the post stops distributing. A strong body with a weak hook is still a failed post.

The Monthly Content Planner
Answer three questions about your last 30 days of client work. Get a full month of post topics — mapped to your content pillars, sequenced for pipeline.
OPEN
When to use At the start of each month — or whenever you feel like you're running out of things to post. Run this once and you'll have more ideas than you can publish. Takes under 10 minutes of input.
The Prompt
I want to plan my LinkedIn content for the next 30 days. Here's what happened in my consulting work this month: Most useful thing I told a client: [describe it in 2-4 sentences] Most interesting objection I heard in a sales conversation: [quote it or paraphrase it] The thing I wish my ideal clients understood before they get on a call with me: [one honest sentence] A framework I used repeatedly this month: [describe it briefly] Something I changed my mind about or saw differently this month: [one observation] My ICP: [fractional CMOs / GTM advisors / etc.] I post 3x per week (Tue/Wed/Thu). This month has [X] posting weeks. Using these inputs, generate a content plan: - Suggest 12 post topics (one per post slot) - For each topic: name the post type (teaching / opinion / story / belief shift / handraiser), the core angle in one sentence, and which input it draws from - Sequence them so the month builds — authority posts early, handraiser post in week 3 - Vary the post types across the week (never two opinion posts back to back) - Flag which 2 topics have the highest pipeline potential and explain why
Pro Tip The three questions at the top of this prompt — most useful thing, objection heard, what clients should know — are the same three inputs that power a professional extraction session. If the output feels sharper than your usual content, that's why.

The DM Opener Generator
Turn a prospect's LinkedIn profile or recent post into three personalised, non-generic DM openers. No pitch, no template language.
OPEN
When to use Before sending a connection request to a high-fit prospect, or when following up after they comment on your post. Paste in what you know about them — LinkedIn About section, a recent post they wrote, or their headline. Get an opener that doesn't read like every other DM they've received.
The Prompt
I want to open a LinkedIn DM conversation with a prospect. Here's what I know about them: Their headline or role: [paste from their profile] Their LinkedIn About section or a recent post they wrote: [paste the relevant text] Why I think they're a good fit for what I do: [one honest sentence — not their job title, but a situation signal] What I do (brief): [1-2 sentences] Write 3 DM openers. Rules: - Each opener is 2-3 sentences maximum - No pitch, no offer, no "I help people like you" - Each one should reference something specific from their profile or post — not generic praise - The goal of the opener is to start a genuine conversation, not to sell - End each opener with a single question that invites them to talk about their situation - Label each opener: Opener A (references their content), Opener B (references their situation), Opener C (references a shared observation) - Human tone — sounds like a message from a peer, not a sales sequence
Pro Tip Before you send any DM, ask yourself: "Would this message make sense if they'd never heard of me?" If the answer is no — if it only works because you've built context about your offer — rewrite it. The opener earns the right to a conversation. It doesn't start the sales process.

The Raw Note Converter
Turn a half-formed thought, a voice memo transcript, or a messy brain dump into a structured LinkedIn post. No idea is too rough for this one.
OPEN
When to use When you have a thought worth sharing but no energy to shape it. Paste in whatever you have — a voice memo transcript, three disconnected sentences, a paragraph you wrote and abandoned. This prompt structures it without changing your voice.
The Prompt
I have a rough idea for a LinkedIn post. Here it is in its current state — unpolished, possibly incomplete: [PASTE YOUR RAW NOTES, BRAIN DUMP, VOICE MEMO TRANSCRIPT, OR HALF-FORMED THOUGHT] My ICP: [fractional CMOs / GTM advisors / etc.] Post type preference (or write "you decide"): [teaching / opinion / story / belief shift] Turn this into a complete LinkedIn post. Requirements: - Do not change my core point or conclusion — restructure how it's expressed, not what it says - Keep my language where it's specific and clear — only rewrite where it's vague or structurally weak - Open with the sharpest version of my main point — not background - Keep paragraphs to 2-3 sentences maximum - Close with a declarative statement, not a question - No em dashes, no exclamation marks, no emojis - After the post, give me one sentence explaining the structural choice you made and one suggestion for how I could make it 10% stronger.
Pro Tip The constraint "do not change my core point" is the most important line in this prompt. Without it, Claude will sharpen your idea into something generic. With it, the output sounds like you — just more structured.
